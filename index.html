Overview

Abstract

With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence and embedded technology implants, brain and body chip implants have become increasingly important in both healthcare and human enhancement. These technologies are designed to improve medical conditions, restore lost abilities, and support daily human activities through intelligent systems integrated within the body.


Despite their potential benefits, the adoption of such technologies raises significant ethical, social, and technological concerns related to privacy, security, autonomy, human identity, and health risks.


This research proposes a model including technological, cognitive, and social factors — perceived privacy, perceived usefulness, perceived risk, trust, subjective norm, and attitude — to examine individuals' willingness to adopt ETI. Data were collected from 175 respondents across Bahrain and analyzed through statistical hypothesis testing.

What We Set Out to Do

Research Objectives

01

Ethical Principles in Implant Use

Explore ethical principles related to brain and body chip implants — focusing on privacy, autonomy, human identity, and fairness — including how data is collected, stored, and used, and whether individuals retain control over their decisions.

02

Factors Driving Adoption

Identify the key motivators for adopting chip implants: medical needs, disability restoration, convenience, and enhanced security — while examining how social acceptance, cultural beliefs, and trust shape individual decisions.

Conceptual Framework

Research Model

The study proposes a model that integrates six key factors influencing individuals' attitudes toward and willingness to use Embedded Technology Implants (ETI).

🔒

Perceived Privacy

Concerns about how personal data collected by implants is stored, accessed, and controlled.

⚡

Perceived Usefulness

Belief that using ETI would enhance performance, health outcomes, and daily life quality.

⚠️

Perceived Risk

Awareness of potential physical, social, and security risks associated with implant adoption.

🤝

Trust

Confidence in the technology, providers, and institutions managing implantable devices.

👥

Subjective Norm

Social influence and peer or community pressure that shapes one's attitude toward implants.

🧠

Attitude Toward ETI

Overall positive or negative evaluation of using embedded technology, directly predicting usage intention.

Results

Key Findings

175 Valid Responses
BH Study Region
Quantitative Research Method
PLS-SEM Analysis Method

Trust significantly influences users' attitudes toward Embedded Technology Implants. Higher trust in technology and institutions leads to more positive adoption intentions.

Perceived Usefulness is a strong predictor of adoption attitude. Respondents who see practical health or daily-life benefits are more willing to accept implants.

Ease of Use positively shapes attitudes — the more intuitive and manageable the technology is perceived, the higher the willingness to use it.

Perceived Risk significantly and negatively influences attitude. Concerns about data breaches, health complications, and misuse reduce adoption willingness.

Attitude strongly predicts Willingness to Use ETI, confirming that overall evaluation of the technology is the primary driver of adoption intent.

The findings highlight an urgent need for clear ethical guidelines, stronger privacy protections, and responsible implementation strategies before widespread adoption in Bahraini society.
