Overview

Abstract

With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence and embedded technology implants, brain and body chip implants have become increasingly important in both healthcare and human enhancement. These technologies are designed to improve medical conditions, restore lost abilities, and support daily human activities through intelligent systems integrated within the body.


Despite their potential benefits, the adoption of such technologies raises significant ethical, social, and technological concerns related to privacy, security, autonomy, human identity, and health risks.


This research proposes a comprehensive model incorporating technological, cognitive, social, and emotional variables to examine individuals' willingness to adopt ETI. Data were collected from 175 respondents across Bahrain and analyzed through multiple linear regression statistical hypothesis testing[cite: 14, 15, 604].

What We Set Out to Do

Research Objectives

01

Ethical Principles in Implant Use

Explore ethical principles related to brain and body chip implants — focusing on privacy, autonomy, human identity, and fairness — including how data is collected, stored, and used, and whether individuals retain control over their decisions[cite: 114, 115].

02

Factors Driving Adoption

Identify the key motivators for adopting chip implants: medical needs, disability restoration, convenience, and enhanced security — while examining how social acceptance, cultural beliefs, and trust shape individual decisions[cite: 120, 121, 122].

Conceptual Framework

Research Model

The study proposes a robust structural model integrating ten key technological, cognitive, social, and emotional factors influencing individuals' attitudes toward and willingness to use Embedded Technology Implants (ETI).

🔒

Perceived Privacy

Concerns representing the fear of losing control over personal information collected by Embedded Technology Implants[cite: 359].

💪

ETI Self-Efficacy

Reflects how confident a person feels in their ability to operate and manage Embedded Technology Implants without help.

🛡️

Technology Safety

The level of protection users believe the implant provides against physical harm, malfunction, or long-term negative effects.

🏥

Health Concern

Worries individuals may have about how Embedded Technology Implants could affect their physical well-being.

👥

Subjective Norm

The influence of people around the individual, such as family, friends, or society, on their decision to use implants[cite: 388].

😨

Anxiety

Feelings of fear, discomfort, or uneasiness that individuals may experience when thinking about implants.

🤝

Perceived Trust

The level of confidence individuals have in the reliability, security, and proper functioning of the implants[cite: 399].

⚡

Perceived Usefulness

Belief that using ETI will provide benefits, enhance performance, and improve their daily life quality[cite: 404].

📱

Perceived Ease of Use

How simple and effortless individuals believe the embedded technology will be to interact with and operate[cite: 409].

⚠️

Perceived Risk

The potential negative outcomes associated with implants, such as safety concerns, health effects, or data misuse[cite: 414].

Results

Key Findings

11/14 Hypotheses Supported
175 Valid Responses
Quantitative Research Method
Regression Analysis Method

Out of the 14 proposed hypotheses in the research model, 11 hypotheses were statistically significant and supported by the multiple linear regression analysis.

Perceived Privacy significantly reduces perceived trust and increases perceived risk. Individuals concerned about personal data are less likely to trust the technology[cite: 611, 612].

Embedded Technology Self-Efficacy showed a strong positive influence. Confidence in using the implants directly increases perceived ease of use and usefulness[cite: 614, 615].

Technology Safety acts as a strong motivator. When users believe the technology is safe, it positively affects perceived usefulness and mitigates perceived risk[cite: 618, 619].

Interestingly, Subjective Norm and Anxiety did not significantly influence attitudes. Participants formed their attitudes independently rather than being swayed by social pressure or emotional fear[cite: 623, 624, 625].

Attitude strongly predicts Willingness to Use ETI, representing the strongest relationship in the research model, confirming that overall evaluation of the technology is the primary driver of adoption intent[cite: 631, 632].
